“The Moment I Realized AI Education Is Broken — and What I Wanted Instead”
During my final semester, I made a deliberate decision:
I wanted to pivot into backend engineering and actually become employable in the real industry — not just “pass courses.” A friend from the University of Waterloo, who had interned at NVIDIA, told me bluntly:

“If you want to be good, you need to learn the real stuff on your own.”

So I started grinding.

I set up three LLMs side‑by‑side — Copilot 365 as my main tutor, and two additional Copilot chats for follow‑ups and deeper clarifications. I followed a curriculum, asked questions, cross‑checked explanations, and tried to build a coherent understanding of backend engineering.

And honestly?
It was one of the most cumbersome, fragmented, and mentally draining learning experiences I’ve ever had.

Every concept required:

switching chats

repeating context

re‑explaining what I already knew

manually stitching together explanations

trying to maintain a consistent learning path

It felt like juggling three different teachers who didn’t talk to each other.

That’s when a harsh realization hit me:

Four years of university hadn’t prepared me for industry-level Computer Science.  
Not because I didn’t learn — but because academic CS is not designed to produce engineers who can ship production systems. It’s designed to follow a curriculum, not to build employability.

So I asked myself:

Why isn’t there one AI that can teach me everything I need — properly, coherently, and continuously?
Not three chats.
Not scattered explanations.
Not a patchwork of prompts.

But one unified, “god‑level” AI educator that:

understands my background

remembers my progress

builds a personalized curriculum

adapts to my questions

teaches visually, interactively, and intelligently

and ultimately makes me a strong, employable CS professional

That frustration — and that vision — became the seed of my dream project:
a Jarvis‑style AI educator that finally fixes the broken way we learn technical skills today.
